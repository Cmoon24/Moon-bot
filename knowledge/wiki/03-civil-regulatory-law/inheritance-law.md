# Inheritance Law

**Inheritance Law** in Thailand is primarily governed by the Civil and Commercial Code (CCC) Book 6. It dictates how a person's estate—comprising assets, rights, and liabilities—is distributed after their death.

## Fundamentals

### The Estate (กองมรดก)
The estate includes all assets, rights, duties, and liabilities of the decedent, except those which are strictly personal by law or nature.
- **Section 1601:** Heirs are only liable for the decedent's debts to the extent of the property they inherit.

### Automatic Transfer
Inheritance occurs at the moment of death. The estate passes to the heirs immediately by operation of law (Section 1599).

## Mechanisms of Distribution

### 1. By Law (Statutory Inheritance)
If no will exists, the estate is distributed among [[statutory-heir|Statutory Heirs]] based on bloodline and marriage.
- Classes of heirs: Descendants, Parents, Siblings, Grandparents, Uncles/Aunts.
- The principle of "near relatives exclude remote" applies, with the exception that children and parents inherit together.

### 2. By Will (Testamentary Inheritance)
A person may dispose of their assets through a [[will-testament|Will]].
- A will can name specific beneficiaries (legatees) for specific assets or the entire estate.
- Testamentary disposition takes precedence over statutory distribution for the assets mentioned.

## Loss of Rights
- **Disinheritance:** Expressly excluding an heir.
- **Forfeiture:** Removal of an heir due to criminal acts against the decedent.
- **Renunciation:** Voluntary refusal of the inheritance by the heir.

## Administration
When disputes arise or complex assets exist, an [[estate-administrator]] is usually appointed by the court to oversee the collection and distribution of assets and settlement of debts.
